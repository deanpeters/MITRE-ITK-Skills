# ITK: Rose, Bud, Thorn

Help a product practitioner apply **Rose, Bud, Thorn** from the MITRE Innovation Toolkit.

**Phase:** EVALUATE · **Difficulty:** Beginner · **Group:** 4+ people · **Time:** 45+ minutes

> Visually categorize positive (rose), potential (bud), or negative (thorn) aspects of a topic (e.g., system, product, process).

---

## When This Tool Fits Best

- Sprint retrospectives to surface systemic friction and emerging opportunities before committing to the next roadmap cycle
- Early discovery sessions where stakeholders disagree on what's working and where the product should head next
- Post-launch reviews to categorize what delighted users, what shows promise, and what needs immediate fixing
- Stakeholder workshops aligning cross-functional teams on a legacy system's strengths and pain points before modernization
- Quarterly product health checks that need a fast, low-friction way to inventory wins, bets, and risks

---

## Key Concepts

**Rose** — A current strength or positive aspect that is working well and worth preserving. Identifying roses prevents teams from breaking what's valuable during change or de-prioritizing proven features.

**Bud** — An emerging opportunity or potential with promise but not yet realized. Buds are the raw material for roadmap bets and discovery follow-ups, distinguishing latent possibility from proven value.

**Thorn** — A challenge, pain point, or risk that is actively causing problems. Naming thorns explicitly creates psychological safety to discuss failures and feeds the problem backlog.

**Affinity Clustering** — Grouping related sticky notes into themes after generation to reveal patterns. Clustering converts scattered individual observations into actionable categories that map to epics or workstreams.

**Divergent Capture** — The practice of one idea per sticky note to keep items atomic and re-groupable. This prevents compound statements that conflate multiple issues and resist prioritization.

**Action Translation** — Converting categorized insights into owned action items, backlog entries, or experiments. Without this step the exercise produces sentiment but no commitment, eroding participant trust.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Watch For

- Ending the session with categorized stickies but no assigned owners or backlog entries, so participants conclude their input was ignored and disengage from future workshops.
- Letting dominant voices or HiPPOs anchor the discussion, which suppresses thorns from junior members and produces a falsely positive picture of product health.
- Conflating buds with thorns because facilitators don't define the categories upfront, collapsing the distinction between 'unrealized opportunity' and 'active problem' that drives different responses.
- Writing vague compound notes like 'onboarding is bad' instead of atomic, specific observations, making the items impossible to cluster, prioritize, or convert into stories.
- Treating every thorn as equally urgent without a follow-up prioritization or trimming pass, flooding the backlog with low-impact items and diluting focus on real risks.
- Running it with a homogeneous group of insiders rather than a diverse mix of users, stakeholders, and SMEs, which echoes existing assumptions and misses blind-spot thorns.

---

## How to Help

- **Read context first.** Before asking anything, check what the user has already shared — their role, product, team size, timeline, what phase of work they're in.
- **Experienced users can skip straight to the work.** If they name this tool directly or describe a clear situation, help them run it — don't force a guided flow.
- **Use the Adaptive Decision Ladder when the situation is unclear.** Offer numbered options; the user can reply `2`, `1 & 3`, or describe freely. Skip steps that context already resolves.
- **Walk through the facilitation steps** from the full skill reference, adapting language and examples to their specific product context.
- **Surface pitfalls proactively** — especially those most likely given what they've shared.
- **Suggest complementary tools** when the conversation reveals adjacent needs.
- **Stay grounded in product artifacts**: PRDs, OKRs, roadmaps, user stories, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder briefings.
