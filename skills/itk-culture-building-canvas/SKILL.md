# Culture Building Canvas

## What Is It

A framework based on “influence channels” that shape and define an organization’s culture.

## Why Use It

Build and foster healthy cultures in a respectful, inclusive, transparent manner. Reduce the time needed to introduce such changes, by identifying the initial steps which can be taken right away. Helps teams develop an actionable, specific culture change plan for their organization.

## When to Use It

When trying to establish and foster a new set of shared cultural attributes.

## How to Do It

1. Assemble a small culture team. Give each participant a copy of the Culture Building Canvas to use (ideally on 11×17 paper).
2. Describe the shared beliefs and behaviors that would be found in this culture, filling in the boxes on the left side of the canvas.
3. Write some adjectives in the “What does the Desired Culture look like?” section, such as creative, compliant, collaborative, speed-oriented, risk-averse, thrifty, creative, etc.
4. List people, organizations, or groups that already exhibit the desired culture in the “Where does the Desired Culture already exist?” section.
5. Fill out the four quadrants with specific answers to the italicized prompts. Include existing items that support the desired culture, as well as new items that need to be developed. Write action sentences, such as “Ms. X meets with Group Y and says Z,” or “Increase offerings of Course ABC by X%.”
6. Identify any contradictory messages coming through these channels. Recognize that enthusiasm in one channel can be contradicted by silence in another.
7. Circle the concrete actions in each quadrant that contribute to conveying a consistent, coherent message through all four quadrants.


## Key Concepts

**Influence Channels** — The mechanisms through which culture is actually transmitted — symbols, behaviors, systems, and rituals. Culture change fails when leaders broadcast values verbally but leave these channels unchanged.

**Four Quadrants** — The canvas divides influence into four categories (typically symbols, systems, behaviors, and stories/rituals) so teams design coherent change across all of them rather than relying on a single lever like a mission statement.

**Message Coherence** — The principle that enthusiasm in one channel can be silently contradicted by inaction in another. Culture forms from the consistency of signals, not their volume, so contradictions must be hunted and resolved.

**Action Sentences** — Specific, attributable commitments phrased as 'Person X does Y with Z' rather than abstract aspirations. They convert vague culture goals into observable, accountable behaviors teams can verify.

**Desired Culture Exemplars** — Existing people, teams, or organizations that already embody the target attributes. Borrowing proven patterns shortens change cycles and gives the team concrete models instead of inventing norms from scratch.

**Espoused vs. Enacted Values** — The gap between the values an org claims and the behaviors it rewards. The canvas surfaces this gap by forcing teams to map actual channel signals against stated cultural aspirations.


## PM Applications

- Define the team operating norms section of a team charter or ways-of-working document, translating abstract values like 'customer-obsessed' into specific channel actions.
- Plan culture-related OKRs and key results by converting circled action sentences into measurable initiatives with owners and timelines.
- Facilitate a quarterly retrospective focused on systemic friction, using the four quadrants to diagnose why a stated value (e.g., 'fail fast') isn't being enacted.
- Prepare an executive briefing that proposes a concrete culture-change roadmap, showing leadership which symbols, systems, and rituals need investment.
- Onboard a newly merged or expanded product org by making implicit norms explicit and aligning teams on shared behaviors before roadmap planning.
- Audit incentive and recognition systems during planning to identify where reward structures contradict the discovery or experimentation culture you're promoting.

## Benefits

- Clarifies desired cultural attributes & behaviors, provides material for developing a specific culture building action plan.

## Common Pitfalls

- Filling only the 'symbols and stories' quadrants while ignoring systems and incentives, producing slogans and posters that change nothing measurable.
- Writing vague aspirations like 'be more collaborative' instead of attributable action sentences, leaving no one accountable and no way to verify progress.
- Skipping the contradiction-hunting step, so leadership's verbal enthusiasm gets silently undercut by unchanged promotion criteria or budget allocations.
- Treating the canvas as a one-time offsite artifact rather than revisiting it as behaviors shift, so the plan goes stale and credibility erodes.
- Assembling a culture team of only managers, missing the channels ICs actually experience daily and producing a top-down plan that doesn't land.
- Expecting fast, visible results and abandoning the plan when metrics don't move in a sprint, despite culture change requiring sustained multi-quarter reinforcement.
