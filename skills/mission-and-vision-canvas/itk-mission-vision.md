# ITK: MISsion & Vision Canvas

Help a product practitioner apply **MISsion & Vision Canvas** from the MITRE Innovation Toolkit.

**Phase:** DEFINE · **Difficulty:** Intermediate · **Group:** 6+ people · **Time:** 60+ minutes

> Develop clear statements about your organization’s future aspirations and present activities.

---

## When This Tool Fits Best

- Forming a new product team or pod that needs shared purpose before committing to a roadmap
- Realigning a squad after a strategic pivot when teams disagree about what they're actually building toward
- Translating fuzzy executive ambitions into a concrete mission that anchors quarterly OKRs
- Onboarding a cross-functional team where engineering, design, and business hold conflicting definitions of success
- Pre-work before a portfolio or roadmap review where product purpose must be articulated to stakeholders

---

## Key Concepts

**Vision Statement** — An aspirational description of the future state the product seeks to create, written in directional, time-independent terms. It sets the north star that prioritization and roadmap trade-offs are measured against.

**Mission Statement** — A concrete description of what the team does today, for whom, and how. It grounds daily backlog and sprint decisions in present-tense activity rather than aspiration.

**Vision–Mission Linkage** — The explicit connection showing how current activities (mission) advance the desired future (vision). A weak link is an early signal that the roadmap is busywork disconnected from strategic intent.

**Five Whys** — An iterative questioning technique that drills past surface answers to underlying purpose. Used here to extract the genuine 'why we exist' rather than restating product features.

**Quality Check / Test** — A validation rubric on the canvas used to pressure-test draft statements for specificity, aspiration, and clarity. Prevents shipping vague platitudes that fail to guide real decisions.

**Aspirational vs. Concrete Framing** — The deliberate separation of future-oriented language (vision) from present-tense, measurable language (mission). Conflating the two produces statements that neither inspire nor direct execution.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Watch For

- Wordsmithing the statement to perfection in the first session instead of treating it as an iterative draft — this burns the group's energy on syntax before consensus on substance exists.
- Writing a vision that's actually a mission (or vice versa), producing a 'future aspiration' full of present-day tactics that fails to inspire or a 'mission' so abstract it can't guide sprint decisions.
- Letting the loudest voice or HiPPO dictate the wording, which yields a statement the team nominally signs off on but doesn't actually believe or use in trade-off discussions.
- Filling the canvas boxes with feature lists and KPIs rather than purpose, so the resulting statements describe what you ship instead of why the product exists.
- Skipping the Five Whys and accepting first-pass answers, leaving the real underlying purpose unexamined and the statement interchangeable with any competitor's.
- Treating the finished canvas as a one-time poster, never revisiting it after pivots or annual planning, so it silently decouples from the actual roadmap and OKRs.

---

## How to Help

- **Read context first.** Before asking anything, check what the user has already shared — their role, product, team size, timeline, what phase of work they're in.
- **Experienced users can skip straight to the work.** If they name this tool directly or describe a clear situation, help them run it — don't force a guided flow.
- **Use the Adaptive Decision Ladder when the situation is unclear.** Offer numbered options; the user can reply `2`, `1 & 3`, or describe freely. Skip steps that context already resolves.
- **Walk through the facilitation steps** from the full skill reference, adapting language and examples to their specific product context.
- **Surface pitfalls proactively** — especially those most likely given what they've shared.
- **Suggest complementary tools** when the conversation reveals adjacent needs.
- **Stay grounded in product artifacts**: PRDs, OKRs, roadmaps, user stories, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder briefings.
