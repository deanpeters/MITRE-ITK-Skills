# ITK: Quickstart Stakeholder Engagement Canvas

Help a product practitioner apply **Quickstart Stakeholder Engagement Canvas** from the MITRE Innovation Toolkit.

**Phase:** SCOPE · **Difficulty:** Intermediate · **Group:** 1+ people · **Time:** 60+ minutes

> A quick way to begin developing a plan for effectively engaging a stakeholder.

---

## When This Tool Fits Best

- Preparing to engage a powerful executive sponsor whose support is critical but whose motivations are unclear
- Planning outreach to a resistant or skeptical stakeholder who could block your roadmap or release
- Onboarding a newly assigned stakeholder mid-project where relationship history and influence are unknown
- Aligning engagement strategy across a cross-functional team for a high-stakes, politically sensitive initiative
- Designing inclusive engagement for marginalized or underrepresented user communities affected by your product

---

## Key Concepts

**Stakeholder Motivations vs. Your Motivations** — The canvas deliberately separates what the stakeholder needs and wants from what you need from them, exposing misalignment before it derails engagement. Mapping both sides prevents one-directional persuasion that ignores the stakeholder's actual interests.

**Equity Lens** — An embedded prompt to consider access, power imbalances, and historically excluded voices when planning engagement. It forces teams past their default stakeholder set toward those impacted but rarely consulted.

**Proxy** — A representative who speaks for or stands in for a stakeholder who is unavailable, hard to reach, or represents a group. Identifying a credible proxy keeps engagement moving without misrepresenting the actual stakeholder's interests.

**Analysis vs. Engagement Sides** — The canvas is two-phased: Analysis assesses who the stakeholder is and what drives them; Engagement converts that understanding into a concrete action plan. Skipping Analysis produces engagement tactics with no diagnostic foundation.

**Decide Box (Next Action)** — The closing commitment that names owner, action, medium, timing, key message, and desired outcome for the next touchpoint. It turns reflection into an accountable, scheduled engagement rather than vague intent.

**Influence and Impact** — The degree to which a stakeholder can affect your effort's success and the degree to which your effort affects them. Distinguishing the two prevents over-investing in loud-but-low-influence voices while neglecting quietly powerful ones.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Watch For

- Filling out only 'Your Motivations' and treating the canvas as a persuasion script, producing engagement plans that ignore what the stakeholder actually wants and breeding resistance.
- Running it on every stakeholder regardless of influence, burning hours on low-stakes contacts while the canvas-per-stakeholder cost crowds out actual engagement.
- Completing the Analysis side but never flipping to Engagement, leaving rich insight with no owner, action, or scheduled next step.
- Treating the equity lens as a checkbox rather than acting on it, so the engagement plan still routes around the marginalized voices it surfaced.
- Substituting a convenient proxy for a hard-to-reach decision-maker without validating alignment, leading you to engage someone who can't actually represent the stakeholder's interests.
- Capturing motivations once and never updating them as the project evolves, so engagement decisions rest on stale assumptions about influence and priorities.

---

## How to Help

- **Read context first.** Before asking anything, check what the user has already shared — their role, product, team size, timeline, what phase of work they're in.
- **Experienced users can skip straight to the work.** If they name this tool directly or describe a clear situation, help them run it — don't force a guided flow.
- **Use the Adaptive Decision Ladder when the situation is unclear.** Offer numbered options; the user can reply `2`, `1 & 3`, or describe freely. Skip steps that context already resolves.
- **Walk through the facilitation steps** from the full skill reference, adapting language and examples to their specific product context.
- **Surface pitfalls proactively** — especially those most likely given what they've shared.
- **Suggest complementary tools** when the conversation reveals adjacent needs.
- **Stay grounded in product artifacts**: PRDs, OKRs, roadmaps, user stories, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder briefings.
