# ITK: Bodystorming

Help a product practitioner apply **Bodystorming** from the MITRE Innovation Toolkit.

**Phase:** GENERATE · **Difficulty:** Intermediate · **Group:** 6+ people · **Time:** 45+ minutes

> In this physical variant on brainstorming, participants use their body and inexpensive materials or objects to role-play and mimic interaction with a system, product, or experience.

---

## When This Tool Fits Best

- Validating physical or spatial product interactions before committing engineering resources to hardware or kiosk designs
- Discovery sprints where the team needs embodied empathy for a workflow they haven't personally experienced
- Testing multi-actor service flows where humans, systems, and handoffs interact across touchpoints
- Early concept stages when sketches and wireframes fail to expose physical friction or sequencing problems
- Onboarding a team to a new user segment by walking through their lived experience end-to-end

---

## Key Concepts

**Embodied Cognition** — The principle that physically acting out an interaction surfaces insights the mind misses when reasoning abstractly. Moving through a scenario reveals friction, sequencing errors, and ergonomic constraints that static artifacts hide.

**Touchpoint** — Each discrete moment a user interacts with the system, product, or service. Bodystorming forces you to enumerate and physically rehearse every touchpoint, exposing gaps in the end-to-end journey.

**Risky Design** — A concept that is expensive or risky to build but cheap to fake with cardboard and props. Bodystorming lets you stress-test these designs at near-zero cost before any real investment.

**Role Assignment** — Casting participants as actors in the scenario, including non-human roles like a software component or a database. This makes invisible system behavior tangible and reveals dependencies between actors.

**Props as Proxies** — Using inexpensive objects (chairs as seats, books as monitors) to stand in for real components. Low-fidelity proxies keep the focus on interaction flow rather than polish, encouraging experimentation.

**Service Choreography** — The sequenced orchestration of actors, systems, and handoffs across a service. Physically rehearsing it exposes timing problems, dead ends, and ownership gaps that a service blueprint may only imply.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Watch For

- Treating it as improv theater without a defined scenario or capture mechanism, so vivid insights evaporate and never reach the PRD or backlog.
- Over-investing in prop fidelity, which shifts focus to set design instead of interaction flow and defeats the cheap, throwaway intent of the method.
- Casting only product team members and skipping real or representative users, producing an empathy echo chamber that confirms existing assumptions.
- Omitting non-human roles like systems or data, so critical dependencies and handoff failures stay invisible and resurface as defects in development.
- Skipping the structured debrief on what went well, challenges, and opportunities, leaving the team with a fun activity but no prioritized, actionable findings.
- Bodystorming purely digital interfaces with no physical or spatial dimension, where a clickable prototype or wireframe walkthrough would yield faster, cheaper insight.

---

## How to Help

- **Read context first.** Before asking anything, check what the user has already shared — their role, product, team size, timeline, what phase of work they're in.
- **Experienced users can skip straight to the work.** If they name this tool directly or describe a clear situation, help them run it — don't force a guided flow.
- **Use the Adaptive Decision Ladder when the situation is unclear.** Offer numbered options; the user can reply `2`, `1 & 3`, or describe freely. Skip steps that context already resolves.
- **Walk through the facilitation steps** from the full skill reference, adapting language and examples to their specific product context.
- **Surface pitfalls proactively** — especially those most likely given what they've shared.
- **Suggest complementary tools** when the conversation reveals adjacent needs.
- **Stay grounded in product artifacts**: PRDs, OKRs, roadmaps, user stories, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder briefings.
