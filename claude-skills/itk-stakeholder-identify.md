# ITK: Stakeholder Identification Canvas

Help a product practitioner apply **Stakeholder Identification Canvas** from the MITRE Innovation Toolkit.

**Phase:** SCOPE · **Difficulty:** Beginner · **Group:** 2+ people · **Time:** 60+ minutes

> Ideate a more comprehensive & representative set of relevant stakeholders to your project.

---

## When This Tool Fits Best

- Early discovery when you need to map who benefits or gets harmed before committing to a solution scope
- Pre-engagement planning to surface marginalized or non-obvious stakeholders before building a stakeholder engagement strategy
- Kicking off a new initiative where conflicting priorities require an inclusive, equity-aware view of who matters
- Designing for regulated or public-sector products where indirect and downstream impacts carry real consequences
- Re-scoping a project that stalled because key affected groups were overlooked in the original requirements

---

## Key Concepts

**Customers, Connectors, Audience** — A categorization scheme separating direct users (customers), intermediaries who spread or influence (connectors), and those observing or affected (audience). Sorting stakeholders this way clarifies engagement priority and influence pathways.

**R/P/D Marking** — Tagging stakeholders as providers of Resources, Permission, or Decision-making authority. This quickly reveals who can fund, block, or approve your initiative versus who is merely interested.

**Build Empathy** — Deliberately considering who experiences a difference or consequence from your effort. It shifts stakeholder thinking from org-chart roles to lived impact, surfacing needs that requirements would otherwise miss.

**Notice Bias & Assumptions** — Explicitly naming the team's blind spots about who counts as a stakeholder. Without this step, teams default to familiar, well-resourced voices and exclude underrepresented or edge-case populations.

**Primary, Secondary, Tertiary Effects** — Tracing ripple effects of your product outward from direct users to indirectly impacted groups. Considering these orders of effect produces a more comprehensive and defensible stakeholder list.

**Equity Lens** — A facilitation discipline of stretching the team to include those at the margins or edges who are typically underrepresented. It drives more equitable product outcomes rather than optimizing only for powerful stakeholders.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Watch For

- Treating the initial 'who are all the stakeholders' brainstorm as the final list instead of expanding it during categorization, producing a shallow, default-biased set.
- Running the canvas without a facilitator skilled in the equity lens, so the team only names familiar, well-resourced stakeholders and misses marginalized groups.
- Confusing the categorization step with prioritization and engaging everyone equally, which dilutes effort instead of focusing on the 2-3 stakeholders to understand deeply.
- Skipping the 'Notice Bias & Assumptions' prompt, leaving unexamined blind spots that surface later as missed requirements or harmed user groups.
- Generating a comprehensive list but capturing no concrete next-step actions, so the canvas becomes a static artifact that never informs engagement strategy.
- Listing roles or org units instead of actual people and impacted communities, which keeps empathy abstract and weakens downstream research targeting.

---

## How to Help

- **Read context first.** Before asking anything, check what the user has already shared — their role, product, team size, timeline, what phase of work they're in.
- **Experienced users can skip straight to the work.** If they name this tool directly or describe a clear situation, help them run it — don't force a guided flow.
- **Use the Adaptive Decision Ladder when the situation is unclear.** Offer numbered options; the user can reply `2`, `1 & 3`, or describe freely. Skip steps that context already resolves.
- **Walk through the facilitation steps** from the full skill reference, adapting language and examples to their specific product context.
- **Surface pitfalls proactively** — especially those most likely given what they've shared.
- **Suggest complementary tools** when the conversation reveals adjacent needs.
- **Stay grounded in product artifacts**: PRDs, OKRs, roadmaps, user stories, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder briefings.
