# ITK: Lotus Blossom

Help a product practitioner apply **Lotus Blossom** from the MITRE Innovation Toolkit.

**Phase:** GENERATE · **Difficulty:** Beginner · **Group:** 2+ people · **Time:** 60+ minutes

> Focus the power of brainstorming using a structured, visual representation of ideas—pushing you to fill out every box with new ideas.

---

## When This Tool Fits Best

- Expanding a vague feature theme into 64 concrete solution candidates before backlog refinement narrows scope
- Discovery workshops where the team keeps defaulting to obvious features and needs forced divergent thinking
- Decomposing a strategic OKR objective into sub-themes and tactics across multiple owners
- Exploring the full facet space of a user problem before committing to a JTBD hypothesis
- Generating a wide opportunity set for a roadmap brainstorm when leadership wants options, not a single bet

---

## Key Concepts

**Idea Propagation** — The mechanic of moving the eight surrounding ideas outward to become new center seeds, forcing each first-order idea to spawn its own cluster. This systematically pushes teams past their initial obvious answers into second- and third-order thinking.

**Forced Quantity** — The 64-box structure deliberately demands more ideas than feel natural, defeating premature convergence. The discomfort of filling empty boxes is the feature, not a bug—it surfaces non-obvious options.

**Center Seed** — The problem, theme, or item placed in the middle box that anchors a blossom. Choosing the right altitude for the seed (problem vs. solution vs. theme) determines whether the output is actionable or scattershot.

**Structured Divergence** — A visual brainstorming pattern that bounds free-association inside a grid. Unlike open whiteboarding, it guarantees balanced coverage across eight dimensions instead of over-investing in one popular thread.

**Binning** — Because ideas are organized under eight parent themes from the start, the output arrives pre-categorized—reducing the affinity-mapping and clustering work normally required after a brainstorm.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Watch For

- Putting a solution in the center seed instead of a problem, which locks the team into elaborating one predetermined answer rather than exploring the problem space.
- Treating the 64 boxes as a completion checklist and filling them with low-quality filler just to fill space—dilutes the review stage and buries the few viable ideas.
- Letting one facilitator hold the pen, so the canvas reflects a single person's mental model instead of the team's divergent perspectives; rotate or use parallel sticky notes.
- Skipping the convergence step—generating 64 ideas but never scoring or selecting, leaving an undifferentiated list that never reaches the backlog.
- Choosing the eight first-order themes too narrowly or with overlap, which causes redundant outer blossoms and wastes the propagation mechanic.
- Going too specific too fast in the inner ring, so outer blossoms have no room to expand and the exercise yields shallow variations rather than genuinely new directions.

---

## How to Help

- **Read context first.** Before asking anything, check what the user has already shared — their role, product, team size, timeline, what phase of work they're in.
- **Experienced users can skip straight to the work.** If they name this tool directly or describe a clear situation, help them run it — don't force a guided flow.
- **Use the Adaptive Decision Ladder when the situation is unclear.** Offer numbered options; the user can reply `2`, `1 & 3`, or describe freely. Skip steps that context already resolves.
- **Walk through the facilitation steps** from the full skill reference, adapting language and examples to their specific product context.
- **Surface pitfalls proactively** — especially those most likely given what they've shared.
- **Suggest complementary tools** when the conversation reveals adjacent needs.
- **Stay grounded in product artifacts**: PRDs, OKRs, roadmaps, user stories, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder briefings.
